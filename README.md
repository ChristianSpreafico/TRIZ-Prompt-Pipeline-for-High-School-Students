# TRIZ Prompt Pipeline for High School Students

An educational collection of AI prompts designed to introduce **TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving)** through a guided, interactive, and project-based learning experience.

---

# Why This Project?

Have you ever wondered how engineers create products that are faster, lighter, safer, cheaper, and more efficient?

Imagine you are asked to improve a common product, such as a hairdryer, a bicycle, or a washing machine.

Very quickly, you discover that improving one feature often makes another one worse.

* A hairdryer that dries faster may consume more electricity.
* A bicycle that is lighter may become less durable.
* A washing machine that cleans better may require more water.

These situations are common in engineering and product design. They are not solved by guessing or waiting for inspiration—they are solved through **structured problem solving**.

This repository introduces one of the world's most well-known innovation methodologies: **TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving)**.

Instead of asking students to memorize theory, the project guides them through the same reasoning process used by engineers to analyze technical systems, identify problems, and develop innovative solutions.

Using a sequence of carefully designed AI prompts, students progressively build a complete engineering analysis while learning the fundamental concepts of TRIZ in a practical and interactive way.

The objective is not simply to obtain an answer from an AI assistant, but to learn **how to think systematically**, ask the right questions, and justify engineering decisions through logical reasoning.

# What Is TRIZ?

**TRIZ** stands for **Theory of Inventive Problem Solving**.

It is a systematic methodology for innovation developed by the Soviet engineer **Genrich Altshuller** after analyzing hundreds of thousands of patents from many different engineering disciplines.

While studying these inventions, Altshuller made an important observation: truly innovative solutions rarely appeared by chance. Instead, many successful inventions solved similar types of problems, even if they belonged to completely different industries.

This discovery led to a fundamental idea:

> **Innovation often follows recognizable patterns.**

Rather than depending only on creativity, intuition, or brainstorming, engineers can use these patterns to solve problems in a logical and repeatable way.

Based on this research, Altshuller and his collaborators developed TRIZ—a collection of analytical tools that help engineers understand technical problems, identify contradictions, exploit available resources, and systematically generate innovative solutions.

Unlike traditional brainstorming, TRIZ does not ask, *"What ideas can we think of?"*

Instead, it asks questions such as:

* What is the real problem?
* Which part of the system should be improved?
* What prevents that improvement?
* Which contradiction must be resolved?
* Which existing resources can be exploited?
* What would the ideal solution look like?

By answering these questions step by step, engineers can transform complex and confusing problems into structured models that are much easier to analyze and improve.

This prompt pipeline follows the same philosophy. Rather than providing immediate answers, it guides students through the TRIZ reasoning process, allowing them to understand not only **what** solution is proposed, but also **why** that solution makes sense.

# Fundamental Concepts of TRIZ

To understand how TRIZ works in practice, it is useful to introduce some of its key concepts. These ideas form the foundation of the entire methodology and are used throughout the prompt pipeline.

---

## Technical Systems

In TRIZ, every product or process can be described as a **technical system**.

A technical system is made of different components that interact with each other to perform a specific function or achieve a goal.

Examples of technical systems include:

* Hairdryer
* Bicycle
* Coffee machine
* Washing machine
* Smartphone

Understanding a system means understanding how its parts work together to produce a useful result.

---

## Requirements

Every technical system exists because it satisfies one or more **requirements**.

Examples:

* Dry hair quickly
* Wash clothes effectively
* Transport a person safely and comfortably
* Clean materials efficiently

Identifying the real requirement is the first step in solving any engineering problem, because it defines what the system is actually supposed to achieve.

---

## Contradictions

Many engineering problems arise because improving one aspect of a system makes another aspect worse.

For example:

* Increasing drying speed may increase energy consumption
* Increasing strength may increase weight
* Increasing performance may reduce efficiency

These situations are called **technical contradictions**.

In more complex cases, a single component may need opposite properties at the same time (for example, being both rigid and flexible). These are called **physical contradictions**.

A core idea of TRIZ is that the best solutions do not compromise, but resolve these contradictions.

---

## Resources

TRIZ encourages engineers to first look at what is already available in the system before adding new elements.

These are called **resources**, and they can include:

* Materials
* Energy
* Time
* Space
* Information
* Existing components
* Environmental conditions

Using resources effectively often leads to simpler, cheaper, and more efficient solutions.

---

## Ideality

TRIZ introduces the concept of the **Ideal Final Result (IFR)**.

An ideal system is one that:

* delivers all useful functions
* requires minimal cost
* uses minimal energy and materials
* produces no harmful effects

Although perfect ideal systems rarely exist, this concept helps guide engineers toward better and more efficient solutions by focusing on what the system *should ideally achieve*.

---

## Inventive Principles

TRIZ includes a set of **40 Inventive Principles**, which are general strategies extracted from successful inventions.

Examples include:

* Segmentation
* Dynamization
* Prior Action
* Another Dimension
* Feedback
* Universality

These principles help transform abstract problems and contradictions into concrete solution directions.

# How the Prompt Pipeline Works

The prompt pipeline is organized as a **progressive workflow**.

Each prompt represents one stage of the TRIZ methodology and has a specific role in the problem-solving process.

The general process works as follows:

1. The student opens the first prompt in the sequence.
2. The AI asks structured questions about the technical system and the problem.
3. The student responds step by step.
4. The AI analyzes the answers and organizes the information.
5. The output becomes the input for the next prompt.
6. The process continues until a complete TRIZ analysis is built.

Throughout the pipeline, the AI acts as a guide that helps structure thinking, but it does not replace the student's reasoning.

Instead of giving immediate solutions, it encourages students to:

* define the problem clearly;
* understand system requirements;
* identify contradictions;
* explore constraints and resources;
* generate and evaluate solution concepts.

This iterative structure helps prevent information overload and makes complex engineering reasoning easier to understand.

Each stage builds on the previous one, allowing students to see how a real engineering analysis evolves step by step.

# How to Use the Prompt Pipeline

The prompts in this repository are designed to be used in a **sequential learning flow**.

To start using the pipeline:

1. Open the first prompt file: `01_Problem_Definition.md`
2. Copy its content into your AI assistant (for example ChatGPT)
3. Answer the questions step by step
4. Let the AI guide you through the analysis
5. Move to the next prompt in the sequence
6. Repeat the process until the full TRIZ analysis is completed

Each prompt builds on the information collected in the previous steps. For this reason, it is important to proceed in order and answer carefully.

The quality of the final analysis depends on the consistency and clarity of the responses given throughout the pipeline.

This approach allows students to experience TRIZ not as a static theory, but as a **guided reasoning process**.
